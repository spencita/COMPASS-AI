The Problem

The CCC makes career-defining decisions on incomplete data

Twice a year, faculty determine residents' certification trajectories using fragmented, subjective, and unsynthesized information. The consistency of these decisions has never been formally measured at most programs.

📋

Incomplete Information

CCC members rely on memory and scattered evaluations. Years of clinical notes and structured assessments go unseen by the people deciding milestone ratings and certification readiness.

🎲

Inconsistent Decisions

Inter-rater variability in milestone assignment is a recognized challenge. Two faculty rating the same resident may reach different conclusions — and the gap is never measured or addressed.

💡

Data Already Exists

New Innovations narratives and Epic/Cerner clinical notes contain rich competency signals. No program has used AI to synthesize this data for CCC deliberation — until now.

The Innovation

Human vs. AI — then a personalized plan for every resident

COMPASS-AI independently performs the same assessment task as the CCC, then reveals where AI and faculty agree, and where they diverge — prompting deeper investigation and more informed decisions.

Step 01

Synthesize

COMPASS-AI reads New Innovations evaluation narratives and Epic/Cerner clinical notes through a HIPAA-compliant LLM framework, building longitudinal competency profiles for each resident.

Step 02

Compare

AI-generated milestone recommendations are placed beside faculty CCC determinations. Where they agree, confidence strengthens. Where they diverge, the CCC investigates further.

Step 03

Identify Gaps

Residents at risk of milestone non-attainment are flagged before the CCC meeting, ensuring no competency gap goes unaddressed due to fragmented information review.

Step 04

Individualize

Where gaps are identified, COMPASS-AI generates a structured, personalized learning recommendation — transforming the CCC from a rating exercise into a developmental intervention.

🧭

Why COMPASS?

A compass doesn't make decisions for you. It orients you — pointing toward where you need to go. COMPASS-AI doesn't replace faculty judgment. It gives faculty the clearest possible picture of each resident before they deliberate, so every decision is made with complete, longitudinal, objective evidence.

Competency Framework

What AI Can — and Cannot — Assess

COMPASS-AI is anchored to our program's 17 Entrustable Professional Activities across inpatient and outpatient settings. Scientific honesty about AI's limits is a strength, not a weakness.

Domain What It Means What AI Reads in Notes AI Assessment
PC — Patient Care History-taking, clinical reasoning, diagnosis, and treatment decisions H&P exams, progress notes, discharge summaries — A&P sections reveal clinical reasoning in real time ✓ Strong
MK — Medical Knowledge Understanding of diseases, medications, and diagnostics applied to real patients Consult requests, medication rationale, diagnostic reasoning in notes ✓ Strong
PBLI — Practice-Based Learning Learning from experience, using evidence, incorporating feedback over time How note quality evolves across the year — growth visible in longitudinal documentation ~ Moderate
SBP — Systems-Based Practice Navigating the health system — referrals, care coordination, transitions Discharge planning detail, consult coordination, care transition documentation ~ Moderate
ICS — Interpersonal Communication Verbal communication with patients, families, and the care team Notes hint at communication style — but verbal interactions require direct observation ✕ Excluded
PROF — Professionalism Reliability, ethics, accountability, and appropriate professional behavior Note timeliness is a weak proxy — subjective judgments need direct human observation ✕ Excluded
Study Design

Two components. One year. Validated, then deployed.

A quantitative validation and implementation study at the University of Miami / Jackson Memorial Hospital Internal Medicine Residency Program.

Component 1

Retrospective Validation

Cohortn = 117
  • Three graduating classes — 2024, 2025, 2026 — 39 residents per class
  • Data: New Innovations milestone ratings, EPA assessments, narrative evaluations, stratified sample of inpatient + outpatient clinical notes from Epic and Cerner
  • HIPAA-compliant LLM generates longitudinal competency profiles
  • Concordance with historical CCC decisions measured via Cohen's weighted kappa
  • Discordant cases undergo structured content analysis by competency domain
Component 2

Prospective Deployment

Cohort — AY 2026–2027n = 78
  • 39 PGY-2 and 39 PGY-3 residents in current academic year
  • November 2026 CCC: Standard deliberation + blind COMPASS-AI run. Post-meeting comparison. Baseline kappa established.
  • May 2027 CCC: AI profiles delivered 48 hrs prior as decision-support summaries
  • Primary outcome: change in inter-rater agreement (Cohen's weighted kappa)
  • Secondary: faculty perceptions via pre/post REDCap-based surveys
Project Timeline

6 milestones across 14 months

Built around our two annual CCC meetings in November and May — every milestone advances the science and the tool.

1
September – October 2026
Foundation and Regulatory Setup
IRB submission, institutional data use agreements, Epic and Cerner EMR access scoping with Dr. Suarez, New Innovations data export authorization, REDCap survey design, and biostatistician engagement through UM CTSI.
2
October – November 2026
Retrospective Validation
Export and analysis of three years of de-identified New Innovations data for 117 graduating residents. AI pipeline development and deployment. Concordance analysis against historical CCC milestone determinations using Cohen's weighted kappa.
3
November 2026
CCC Cycle 1 — Blind Comparison
Standard CCC deliberation proceeds as normal. COMPASS-AI independently generates competency profiles from the same data. Results compared post-meeting to establish baseline inter-rater agreement and identify areas of concordance and divergence.
4
December 2026 – April 2027
AI Pipeline Refinement and Faculty Training
Pipeline refined based on retrospective findings. CCC faculty trained on COMPASS-AI tool. Individualized learning recommendation framework finalized. Prospective data collected in real time for the 78-resident cohort.
5
May 2027
CCC Cycle 2 — Live Intervention
COMPASS-AI profiles delivered to CCC members 48 hours prior to deliberation. Faculty deliberate with AI decision-support. Inter-rater agreement measured and compared to baseline. Individualized learning recommendations generated. Pre/post surveys administered via REDCap.
6
June – November 2027
Analysis and Dissemination
Statistical analysis completed. Manuscript submitted to Journal of Graduate Medical Education. Findings presented at ABMS Annual Conference. Abstract submitted to AMIA Annual Symposium (November 2027, San Diego). Open-access implementation toolkit released nationally.
Research Questions

Three questions. Three components. All answerable in one year.

Each research question maps directly to a study component and produces a measurable, publishable outcome.

01
Retrospective Validation

To what extent do AI-generated competency profiles demonstrate concordance with historical Clinical Competency Committee milestone determinations?

02
Prospective CCC Deployment

Does the integration of AI-generated competency profiles into CCC deliberations improve inter-rater agreement among faculty on ACGME milestone ratings compared with standard practice?

03
Individualized Learning

To what extent do AI-generated competency profiles identify individualized learning needs beyond those identified through routine Clinical Competency Committee assessment?

Supporting Evidence

Three pillars of peer-reviewed evidence

COMPASS-AI is built on verified, recent literature across CCC assessment science, AI in GME, and LLM analysis of clinical documentation. Click each citation to expand.

Schaye V, DiTullio D, Sartori DJ et al.
LLM-Based Assessment of Clinical Reasoning Documentation in the EHR Across Two Institutions
Journal of Medical Internet Research · 2025 · doi:10.2196/67967
First multi-institutional study applying large language models to clinical reasoning documentation across two internal medicine residency programs. Establishes the technical feasibility of extracting competency signals from clinical documentation — the foundational capability upon which COMPASS-AI is built.
Sun T, Hamstra SJ, Yamazaki K, Ren KJ, Smith B.
Construct Validity Evidence for ACGME Milestones in Surgical Specialties: A Systematic Review
Journal of Graduate Medical Education · 2025 · 17(2):162–171
Systematic review of 114 studies (2013–2023). Identified inter-rater reliability and rating process variability as persistent challenges. Only 12 of 114 studies reported internal structure validity evidence. Establishes the scientific need for standardized data synthesis in CCC deliberation.
Hauer KE, Vandergrift J, Lipner RS et al.
Reimagining the Clinical Competency Committee to Enhance Education
Journal of General Internal Medicine · 2022
Demonstrated that while CCCs have structural capacity to support individualized learning plan development, this potential is rarely realized due to constraints in the current information environment. Directly motivates the COMPASS-AI intervention.
Janumpally R.
The Role of Natural Language Processing in Graduate Medical Education: A Scoping Review
Cureus · 2025 · doi:10.7759/cureus.81078
Scoping review of NLP applications in GME (2018–2024). Identified automated performance evaluation, competency gap identification, and personalized learning recommendations as emerging areas of impact. Supports the feasibility of NLP-based synthesis of narrative evaluation data at scale.
Kwan BYM et al.
Leveraging LLMs to Evaluate Narrative Feedback Quality for Surgery Residents in CBME
Annals of Surgery Open · 2025 · 6(4):e608
Demonstrated that LLMs can evaluate narrative feedback in EPA assessments with strong performance across multiple feedback quality dimensions. Establishes that LLMs can process structured narrative evaluation data similar to that collected in New Innovations.
